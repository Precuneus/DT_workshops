Question One
The leader reads this aloud.

Everyone else: close your eyes and listen.

"Close your eyes. Take a breath. Now, go to a city center you know well. A real place you've actually been to. You're standing there right now.

Look around. Can you see the buildings? What color are they? Are there shop windows? What's in them? Look down at the pavement. Is it cobblestone? Concrete? Wet?

Now look further. Are there people? How are they moving? Is anyone sitting on a bench? Are there pigeons on the ground? Cars passing by?

Now listen. What do you hear? Traffic? Voices? Music from somewhere? Someone's heels on the pavement? A bus pulling away?

Now feel. What's the air like on your skin? Is it warm or cold? Is there wind? Can you feel the ground under your feet?

Now breathe in. What does it smell like? Coffee from a cafe? Exhaust? Rain? Bread from a bakery?

Stay there for a moment. Look around one more time.

Now open your eyes."

Discuss this for a few minutes. Leader: listen to your group, try to relate to their experience. When you're ready, answer: what felt most important in the imagined experience?

The Science Behind It
What just happened?

The city exercise wasn't random. People genuinely differ in how they imagine. Some see vivid pictures. Some hear sounds first. And some experience pure concepts with no sensory content at all.

These differences are real and measurable. At the extremes:

Aphantasia is the inability to form voluntary mental images. About 2-5% of people experience this. No pictures in the mind's eye, ever.

Hyperphantasia is the opposite: extraordinarily vivid, almost photograph-like mental imagery.

The identity question connects to one of the oldest puzzles in philosophy: the Ship of Theseus. If you replace every plank of a ship, one by one, is it still the same ship? If every cell in your body is replaced, every belief updated, every habit changed... are you still you?

Now you can start thinking about who you are and plan who you will become.
